A Leap of Faith: 5 Ways Kubernetes’ Container-Orchestration System Transforms Existing and Emerging Companies

In the previous decade, DevOps management and workflow platforms have uprooted into mindboggling levels of container-orchestration, Continuous Integration (CI) and Continuous Deployment (CD) services, and deployment automation/testing. IT operators and business administrators are on the verge of pulling their scalp hairs out in response to the overwhelming assortment of app management options and toolkits.

Well, what if this swarm of container services could be tightly wound into an all-in-one solution that conveniently scales app management for efficient multi-program deployment, testing, and releases? Kubernetes (K8s), a powerful open-source system, initially developed by Google, resolves these eye-straining problems by managing containerized applications in a clustered environment, ensuring that your business deploys and releases the most performance-optimized apps into the market.

Yes, we know— as a DevOps administrator or CTO, the sound of “Kubernetes” is probably ringing in your ear as the hype surrounding “container-orchestration”, “pods”, and “cluster management” are slammed into your face by the minute. Nevertheless, Kubernetes’ rise to stardom is without question: Kubernetes’ ability to re-prioritize, automate, and manage container-level applications has transformed collaborative and technical cultures for emerging companies. Before we dive headfirst into the ‘savannah’ of Kubernetes’ container-orchestration framework, we should breakdown the complexities and hurdles of containers alone and how Kubernetes resolves these hurdles.

The ‘Gobbledygook’ of Containers: Transforming Your Company’s App Management Workflow for the Better

At a crude level, containers are a type of technology that makes the process of hosting applications inside portable environments easier and more efficient. Ultimately, containers have dated back to the ‘dinosaur ages’ of the Linux world for nearly a decade. However, it was not until 2013, when Docker was initiated, that containers gained traction with emerging startups and companies. In the sphere of app deployment and release, you can think of containers like a basket filled with OS-leveled dependencies, parameters, and variables. By using the hand-dandy features of containerization, this “basket” ensures that your program’s dependencies transfer across Operating Systems (OS’s) seamlessly without complications. In other words, the same application developed and executed on a macOS PC would run effectively on a Windows OS platform.

And guess what?— it gets better. Containers are configured in a streamlined fashion to build, test, deploy, and redeploy applications on multiple environments, anywhere from a developer’s laptop to an on-premises data center and even the cloud. We can evaluate the advantages of containers in the following lenses:

  • Less Overhead: Containers require and demand fewer system resources than conventional hard-wired virtual machine environments because they don’t include Operating System (OS) images. The result?: A more computationally efficient and scalable work environment that doesn’t deplete system power, so you can juggle multiple app deployments simultaneously.
  • High-Level Portability: Applications executed in a containerized environment can be deployed seamlessly on multiple different operating systems and hardware platforms. IT operators can scrap the ‘rinse and repeat’ process of reconfiguring app environments by leveraging the portability and consistency of a containerized image.
  • Better Consistency: On a collaborative scale, DevOps teams are left secure knowing that applications in containers will run the same, regardless of where they are deployed. For emerging and already-existing companies, this is extremely disruptive: Environments can be cross-exchanged and transferred between team members on the cloud, and testing/development can be executed in seconds.
  • An All-In-One Solution for Development and Testing: When a developer writes, tests, and deploys an application inside a container backbone, the environment does not change at distinct parts of the delivery chain. Well, how is this a gamechanger?: DevOps members, whether one is in San Francisco and the other in the Bahamas, can exchange containers without interfering with the integrity of the application environment.

Well, How Does Kubernetes Change the Game?

After evaluating the scalable deployment, efficiency, and portability features of containers, what more could be improved— right? For many conservative DevOps teams, containers alone seem to be the holy grail of hyper-efficient app management. Sorry to break it to you: there is another stage of container management that could make or break your DevOps infrastructure— and that’s Kubernetes. Kubernetes has risen to prominence as a container-orchestration system that enables developers to automate and simplify their daily container workflow(s) through multi-container management, automation, and testing. Kubernetes’ mission boils down to automating the protocols of deploying, scaling and managing containerized apps on a group (cluster) of servers.

For tech leads, adopting and deploying Kubernetes adds the following business value to your company:

  • Automatically Minimizing Resource Costs: Let’s face it— the most enticing, ‘cherry on top’ feature of any app workflow platform is bare-minimum expenses. Containers function as isolated packages that include everything the application needs to properly execute. Ultimately, multiple containers can share the same OS and network connection, which increases resource efficiency by over ten-fold. In contrast to a physical Virtual Machine (VM), a Kubernetes container ensures that each container runs at maximal performance with the least resource usage.
  • Storage Management: Kubernetes uses a “Container Storage Interface” (CSI) which drastically simplifies storage management and connectivity due to inherent security, update, and framework features. The CSI system takes advantage of an Extensible Plugin Framework which is storage drivers NOT directly hardcoded into Kubernetes. Additionally, the CSI features no-downtime updates by automatically upgrading storage hardware and software. Finally, the CSI is filled to the brim with high security, ensuring that app-level storage is encrypted and locked in your storage drivers.
  • Load Balancing: The Kubernetes backend uses HTTP(S) Load Balancers to target Pods directly and to evenly distribute their traffic to Pods. Kubernetes takes advantage of state-of-the-art traffic management rules, which efficiently split traffic and resources among Pods. K8s uses container-native load balancing, which only directs traffic to Pods (applications) that are operating correctly. In other words, traditional load balancing rules would distribute traffic full-handedly towards all applications, whether running or ‘broken’. However, Kubernetes assesses the health of application deployments and prioritizes resources on successful apps, while limiting traffic towards ‘non-working’ programs.
  • High-Security Standards: By using the dubbed “4C’s rule of Cloud-Native Security (Cloud Datacenter, Cluster, Container, and Code), Kubernetes addresses all levels of the DevOps security hierarchy. Kubernetes works from the top-down, ensuring that data centers and storage solutions are encrypted and that only reputable virtualization platforms are accepted (e.g. Google Cloud Platform, Microsoft Azure, IBM Cloud, and Amazon Web Services).
